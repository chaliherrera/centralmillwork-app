Design principles

The seven rules that govern the entire system. If any one of them is rejected, the design changes.

#PrincipleWhat it meansNotes / objections
P1The target date is sacred The delivery date committed to the client is never recalculated automatically. Internal dates and the projection are recalculated. When the projection passes the target date, the project turns red — the date does not move. Moving it is a recorded human decision: who, when, why, how many days.
P2No milestone closes on a claim — it closes on evidence There is no “it’s done” checkbox. Every milestone closes because the artifact that proves it appeared (a file, a submittal, a client click, a station move), and the system sets the date, not the person.
P3The record is a by-product of the work The drawing submittal goes out from the system, so the date records itself. If someone has to perform an extra task just to report, that design is wrong and will be abandoned within three weeks.
P4Progress is counted, not estimated No human-typed “% complete”. Progress is evidenced sub-milestones completed over total. If it cannot be counted, it is not displayed.
P5Two clocks Backward from the delivery date: the deadline for each milestone. Forward from actual events: where we are going to land. The gap between them is float. Negative float = risk of missing the date.
P6The system pushes early, it does not explain late A schedule that only absorbs delays is a scoreboard of losses. Every milestone approaching its deadline alerts its owner beforehand, and automatically reminds the client when the ball is in their court.
P7Whoever arrives last holds the ball When a milestone has several predecessors, its actual start is the latest of them. The system names which one it was and how many days it cost. No argument.

Risk model

Per milestone, based on float:

StatusMeaning
GREENFloat ≥ 3 working days
YELLOWFloat between 0 and 3 days
REDNegative float: already eating into the delivery date
GRAYNot yet applicable (predecessors unmet)

Per project: the worst status on the critical chain, plus accumulated days of delay and their attribution.

Attribution of lost days

Every milestone that closes late records who the delay belongs to:

Client · GC · Vendor · Estimating · Engineering · Procurement · Production · Logistics · Field · Finance

That running total is the report that answers “why do we deliver late?” at year end with numbers instead of opinions — and it is the documented argument in front of GCs and clients.

Warning: the first report will be uncomfortable. It is a diagnosis of the process, not a control on people.

The honest read: 17% of the journey is instrumented today, and all of it sits in the middle — Materials, Production and QC. Both ends, which is where the weeks are lost, are new ground.

The underlying implication: the system can only measure what happens inside the system. Estimating, Engineering, Logistics and Field work entirely outside it today — Excel, Pytha, Bluebeam, Smartsheets, Pipedrive, OneDrive, email and phone. If they keep working outside, the schedule will know everything about the shop and nothing about the office. The core decision is not technical: it is whether that work moves into the system.

Client portal

Tokenized link access, one per contact — no accounts to create. Every action is attributed to a specific person.

The client can

  • See their project timeline by milestone, with status and dates
  • See what the project is waiting on and who holds the ball — including when it is them
  • Approve / approve as noted / reject: shop drawings, samples, V/E options, change orders
  • Give the final sign-off
  • See fabrication and installation progress photos
  • Download contract, approved drawings, BOL and shipping list

The client cannot see

  • Costs, margins, budgets
  • Vendors or pricing
  • Any internal information

Automatic to the client

A reminder when a milestone they own is approaching or past due, with the explicit impact on the delivery date:

“Shop drawings have been under review for 12 days. Each additional day pushes the October 15 delivery.”

Why it is the highest-impact piece

It turns the “waiting on the client” black hole into a data point with a date, a time and an owner. It accounts for a large share of current delays, and it is the cheapest thing to instrument.

Why this order

  • Stage 1 starts where the data already exists: the engine is proven against reality, not against invented data
  • Stage 2 comes second because it is the largest gain for the least code
  • Stage 3 is the biggest, and it lands once the engine is proven and the habit of using it exists
  • Stages 5 and 6 come last because they block the least: the schedule already works without them, starting at contract instead of at bid
Honest warning: the estimate assumes consistency. If the work is interrupted for weeks to handle production, it stretches proportionally. And it does not include adoption time, which in the areas working outside the system today may weigh more than the development itself.

What this review needs to produce

  • Durations. The ones in this document are estimates. Correct them with each area’s owner.
  • Owners. The official document names 13 roles (Estimator, Estimating Director, Engineer, PM, Procurement Manager, Production Manager, Production Assistant, Production Controller, Logistics Manager, Field Specialist, Office Manager, Financial Manager, CFO). The system today has 9 roles and they do not match. They need to be mapped.
  • Milestones that are missing, unnecessary, or that don’t work this way in practice.
  • Granularity. One schedule per project, or per item / area with rollup to the project?
  • Working calendar. Working days, Central Millwork holidays, shutdown weeks.
  • Phased delivery. Projects delivered by floor or by area with separate dates.
  • Projects without installation. The document contemplates them (p.37). The chain would close at S-04.
  • Change orders. Do they shift the target date automatically, or does that require a decision?
  • Confirmation of the evidence-required principle, understanding that it means moving Engineering and Field work into the system.
Closing note. This map does not invent a process: it transcribes the one Central Millwork already has written down, and turns it into something the system can calculate. Page 5 of the official document already calls for a schedule built backward from the completion date. What is missing is not the idea — it is that this schedule stops living in a spreadsheet nobody updates and starts finding out what is actually happening.
